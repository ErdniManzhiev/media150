Reflection #1

Susan Sontag's "In Plato's Cave" reading makes me reconsider what I am really doing when I take pictures on my phone. Sontag argues that to photograph is to collect the world , to transform experience into an object that can be stored and possessed. I caught myself on that I often take pictures by this logic. I do pictures to remember, to share, to fell that I was present at that moment, that I was here. But, Sontag makes me think: was I truly been present, or I was just trying to capture the moment without actually living it?

Most of my pictures on my phone are simple and ordinary: friends sitting together, the beautiful evening light hitting room's window, a moment in the middle of the volleyball game, a bowl of ramen in a random place. In these moments, the photo, as Sontag tells, becomes a certificate of experience. Like I take photo not only to remember it after, but also confirm that I was a part of the event. For me, as someone who recently moved to the United States, the act of taking picture started to mean a lot more since I realized that I might not be able to be in some places that I known and was a part of. My photo folder contains pictures of my hometown streets, but also the New York views. By collecting these images, I create an anthology of images that I can hold in my pocket - very convenient.

But Sontag also warns us that the act of taking a photo is not innocent. When you taking a picture, you are shaping the reality that people will see in that photo, and then project it onto the real world. For example, when you are photographing your friend, you choose the angle, the lightning, the one moment of their face expression. You might take some pictures, and then choose and save only one that you like. And I think, in that sense, you are shaping the reality. What we choose to show and what we choose to crop out is also an important thing to consider and think about.

To represent our world visually at this moment right now, I would choose to use a contrast. To explain my idea, lets imagine this: on one side of a picture, you see a bright image of a person taking selfies and pictures of a concert or some fancy place, and on other side, you see someone alone, and only the light of their phone screen illuminating them in a dark room. The meaning is that we document and save events on a photo to feel present and active, yet this act itself can isolate us, kind of placing a glass between us and reality.

At the end I want to say that now we see that photography is a strong tool, but also it could numb us. It helps us remember things, people, events, places, whatever we want to remember at the time of taking picture. However, it can also simplify reality, as I said, make as feel numb. One interesting thought that I have right now is: if everyone today take pictures to collect and document moments, then maybe we should try to create images that do mora than that. Fore example, take pictures that can make us, and other people, think and wonder what is happening beyond what we can see.
